01 The Problem

An industry that throws
people at structural failures

Every major international bank operating in China faces the same challenge: NFRA and PBOC — two regulators with overlapping but inconsistent frameworks, hundreds of reports per submission cycle, and source data never designed with regulatory logic in mind.

The default response has always been headcount. More people, more manual checks, more spreadsheet gymnastics. This works until it doesn't — and when it fails, it fails at 11pm before a submission deadline.

Most reporting systems in this space were never architected. They accumulated.

Business data and regulatory logic living in the same tables. Validation entirely manual. Every change requiring IT involvement. Every error requiring the one person who knows where the bodies are buried. When that person leaves, the system doesn't slow down. It stops.

This is not an execution problem. It's an architecture problem. Fixing the execution buys months. Redesigning the architecture buys years.

02 The Principle

Borrowed from aerospace,
applied to banking

Banks and SpaceX's engineering philosophy seem like they belong in different universes. One moves in decades. The other moves in weeks. But in 2022, Musk's five-step process became the blueprint for rebuilding a regulatory reporting system — in strict order, with one discipline: never skip ahead to automation.

1 Make the requirements less dumb ▼
In banks, requirements accumulate like sediment. A senior stakeholder asked for a trigger to regenerate a single branch's reports independently — a reasonable fear, but the wrong solution. Selective reruns would have introduced exactly the data inconsistency the system was built to eliminate. The trigger was never built. Six months later, nobody asked about it again.
Most requirements are answers to fears, not solutions to problems. Find the fear first.
2 Delete the process step ▼
The original system had over a dozen manual maintenance spreadsheets — each a workaround for a different data quality problem, each a single point of failure. They were replaced with six standardised configuration tables. Not because six is better than twelve, but because most of those spreadsheets existed to compensate for problems fixable upstream, or edge cases that had stopped occurring years ago.
Workarounds accumulate faster than problems get solved. Deletion requires courage, not just analysis.
3 Simplify or optimise ▼
The core architecture is a 3+6 data model: three tables for business data, six for regulatory logic. The two layers never touch directly. When regulators changed requirements, changes stayed in the adjustment tables. Core data was never touched. No IT involvement, no code changes, no waiting.
Simplification isn't subtraction. Done right, it's multiplication.
4 Accelerate cycle time ▼
Accelerating cycle time isn't about moving faster — it's about finding out what's wrong sooner. Run the entire pipeline with actual production data, under actual deadline pressure. Every major issue in this system was found this way. The cycle compressed every time, not because the system got luckier, but because each failure made the next fix faster.
Shorten the distance between "something is wrong" and "it's fixed."
5 Automate ▼
Automation is the last step, not the first. Automate a flawed process and you get a faster flawed process. Automate a simplified, tested, clean process and you get something that compounds. By the sixth month, the team had stopped thinking about it as a system they were managing and started thinking about it as infrastructure.
Automate to lock in everything you've already fixed. Success is when nobody has to think about it anymore.

The question
Low-code over Python
Python was rejected not for skill reasons, but because of what Python-based systems look like to the people who inherit them. Finance business users with no technical background learned to maintain parts of this system themselves. That's the difference between a system that outlives its creator and one that doesn't.
The question
Mirror tables over exception systems
A purpose-built exception system would have been cleaner — and would have required IT to build and maintain it. Mirror tables are instantly understandable to anyone who knows the core tables. When upstream data fails at 11pm, the mirror table is the difference between a crisis and a workaround.
The question
Star schema for corporate loans
Customer attributes change infrequently. Loan transactions change constantly. Keeping them in separate tables means customer updates propagate automatically without touching transaction records, and queries don't carry weight they don't need.
The question
PII collapsed into transaction table
An individual customer table would have been architecturally cleaner. It would also have created a centralised, queryable store of personal identity data — a liability that serves no operational purpose. Denormalisation here was a deliberate risk reduction choice.

The system was adopted as the standard template for all future Finance regulatory reporting automation within the bank — extending beyond loans into a full cross-business framework. A long-standing reconciliation failure between NFRA 1104 and EAST reports was resolved as a byproduct of shared data lineage.

When the low-code platform was later discontinued for political reasons, the engineering team didn't revert to manual processes. They went looking for another tool to keep working the same way.

The system outlived its creator. Then it outlived its platform. The methodology is still running.
