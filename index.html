Hi, I'm Jonathan Zhu. Nice to meet you.

I was born in Shanghai in 1989. The kind of kid who'd spend an afternoon with a map, and found the news more interesting than cartoons. That hasn't changed.

I've spent twelve years in financial services — Citi, JPMorgan, Standard Chartered — working across data engineering, regulatory reporting, and systems architecture. I'm not formally trained in any of it. My understanding of technology came from practice: breaking things, fixing them, finding the cleanest solution inside a set of constraints. I've long believed that system failures are almost always architecture problems, not execution problems. That conviction has saved me considerable effort, and made me instinctively skeptical of most "solutions."

Wang Yangming wrote about the unity of knowledge and action. I've taken this seriously: figure out what's right, then act, then think again. The answer lives in the cycle, not before it.

Outside work, my interests aren't systematic, but they're genuine. I follow railways — not just to travel on them, but to read cities and history through their lines. I've followed F1 since the 2003 Austrian Grand Prix. I followed AKB48 for years — less as a fan than as someone watching one of them figure out how to convert cultural capital into a business. She pulled it off, in ways more interesting than most business school case studies. I believe the SpaceX Mars program is less an engineering project than the next reset of human civilisation. I'm interested in military history, political system design, Japanese pop music, Stephen Curry's Warriors, and the kind of reading you do at 3am when you can't quite explain what you're looking for.

In the summer of 2025, I moved with my family to Singapore. It wasn't a plan I'd made years in advance — but when the moment came, it made every kind of sense: a better professional environment, a city that takes education seriously, and a future with more open doors for my son. Arriving here only confirmed it. There's something about this place — the way it's built, the way it functions, the mix of people it attracts — that I find genuinely fascinating. That curiosity became 南洋杂记, where I write about what I notice.
