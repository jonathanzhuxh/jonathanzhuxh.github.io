About Me

Hi, I'm Jonathan Zhu. Nice to meet you.

I was born in Shanghai in 1989. Growing up without the internet, I came to understand the world through encyclopedias, and spent long hours immersed in maps and history books — tracing the layout of places and the logic behind how they came to be. At university I encountered Wang Yangming's doctrine of the unity of knowledge and action, and it became a principle I've followed ever since: think it through, then act; the answer lives in the doing.

I spent twelve years across Citi, JPMorgan, and Standard Chartered, crossing over from the business side to the technical. None of it formally trained — everything I know, I learned by doing: reading requirements, building pipelines, fixing what broke, and getting to the bottom of why. That path gave me something harder to teach: the ability to move freely between business and technology without losing the language of either. Along the way, I came to see banks for what they really are — data companies that happen to provide financial services. The quality of that data, and the architecture built to sustain it, determines almost everything else. Getting that structure right, and keeping it maintainable over time, became the problem I kept returning to.

Outside work, my interests are genuinely varied. History, geography, and politics are my native language — a sensitivity to time, space, and structure that shapes how I read almost everything. My interest in military history centres on tactics and order of battle, not hardware. I follow F1 for the strategy: pit timing, tyre choices, decisions made under pressure. Stephen Curry is my favourite athlete — not just because he changed how basketball is played, but because there's a classically heroic quality to him that goes beyond the highlights. I follow Japanese idol culture as an observer: watching how one of them converted her cultural capital into a sustainable career. She pulled it off — and the story turned out to be more interesting than any business school case study.

In 2025, I moved with my family to Singapore. I've always had a habit when travelling: getting close enough to observe how people actually live, not just what a place shows you. Living here takes that further — and has confirmed a suspicion I already had: much of what passes for commentary on Singapore is embarrassingly shallow. That gap between received opinion and the real thing became Notes from Nanyang — where I write about what I find when I look more carefully.

This gap year has also been something else: a systematic process of taking stock — past projects, the path that led here, and who I am. This website is the part I can make public. It also sharpened a conviction I already held: AI cannot replace people. The story, the taste, the final call on what matters — those still belong to the person. But AI amplifies. The clearer you can think and express yourself, the greater the leverage it gives you.
